# Text Summarization (Extractive)

## Overview
Text Summarization is the process of creating a concise and coherent summary of a longer text document while retaining its most important information. Among the two main types of text summarization, **Extractive Text Summarization** is the simpler and more straightforward approach.

Imagine you have a long article, and you want to quickly grasp its main points without reading the entire thing. Extractive summarization does exactly that by identifying and extracting the most important sentences directly from the original text. It's like highlighting the key sentences in a physical document and then compiling them together to form a summary. The summary generated by an extractive method consists solely of sentences that are present in the original document, without any rephrasing or generation of new words. This makes it highly faithful to the source text.

## What Problem It Solves
In today's information-rich world, we are constantly bombarded with vast amounts of text data – news articles, research papers, legal documents, reports, emails, and more. This sheer volume of information presents several challenges:

1.  **Information Overload:** It's impossible for humans to read and process every piece of text they encounter. This leads to cognitive overload and missed opportunities.
2.  **Time Constraints:** Professionals, students, and general users often need to quickly understand the gist of a document without investing significant time in reading it entirely.
3.  **Decision Making:** Rapid access to key information is crucial for informed decision-making in various fields, from business intelligence to medical diagnosis.
4.  **Content Curation:** For platforms that aggregate content (e.g., news apps, research databases), providing summaries helps users filter and prioritize what they want to read in full.
5.  **Accessibility:** Summaries can make complex or lengthy documents more accessible to a wider audience, including those with reading difficulties or limited time.

Extractive Text Summarization addresses these problems by acting as an intelligent filter. It automatically sifts through lengthy documents, identifies the most salient sentences, and presents them in a condensed format. This allows users to quickly grasp the core message, decide if the full document is worth reading, and save valuable time and effort. It's needed in machine learning because it automates a tedious and time-consuming task that would otherwise require significant human effort.

## How It Works
Extractive text summarization typically follows a pipeline that involves several key steps:

1.  **Text Preprocessing:**
    *   **Sentence Tokenization:** The first step is to break down the input document into individual sentences. This is crucial because extractive summarization operates at the sentence level.
    *   **Word Tokenization:** Each sentence is then broken down into individual words.
    *   **Stop Word Removal:** Common words that carry little meaning (e.g., "the," "a," "is," "are") are often removed to focus on more significant terms.
    *   **Stemming/Lemmatization:** Words are reduced to their root form (e.g., "running," "runs," "ran" become "run") to treat variations of the same word as identical.

2.  **Feature Extraction (Sentence Representation):**
    Each sentence needs to be represented numerically so that its "importance" can be measured. Various features can be extracted:
    *   **Word Frequency/TF-IDF:** Sentences containing words that appear frequently in the document (but are not stop words) or have high TF-IDF scores are often considered more important.
    *   **Sentence Position:** Sentences at the beginning or end of paragraphs/documents often contain introductory or concluding remarks, making them potentially important.
    *   **Named Entities:** Sentences containing a high number of named entities (people, organizations, locations) might be more informative.
    *   **Sentence Length:** Very short or very long sentences might be less informative.
    *   **Keywords/Keyphrases:** Sentences that contain predefined keywords or keyphrases relevant to the topic.
    *   **Similarity to Title/Headings:** Sentences that are highly similar to the document's title or section headings are often central to the topic.
    *   **Cohesion/Connectivity:** How well a sentence connects to other sentences in the document (e.g., using graph-based methods).

3.  **Sentence Scoring:**
    Based on the extracted features, each sentence is assigned a "score" indicating its importance. Common scoring methods include:
    *   **Frequency-based:** Simply counting important words.
    *   **TF-IDF based:** Summing or averaging TF-IDF scores of words within a sentence.
    *   **Graph-based Ranking (e.g., TextRank, LexRank):** This is a popular approach. Sentences are represented as nodes in a graph, and the similarity between sentences (e.g., using cosine similarity of their TF-IDF vectors) forms the edges. An algorithm similar to Google's PageRank is then applied to iteratively calculate a "score" for each sentence, where a higher score indicates greater importance and connectivity within the text.
    *   **Machine Learning Models:** Supervised learning models can be trained to classify sentences as "summary-worthy" or not, given a dataset of documents and their human-generated extractive summaries.

4.  **Sentence Selection:**
    Once all sentences are scored, the top-N highest-scoring sentences are selected to form the summary. The number N can be a fixed count, a percentage of the original document's length, or determined by a threshold score. To improve summary quality, diversity can also be considered, ensuring that selected sentences don't repeat too much information.

5.  **Summary Generation:**
    The selected sentences are then reassembled, typically in their original order of appearance in the document, to maintain coherence and readability.

## Mathematical Intuition

The core of extractive summarization often relies on measuring the "importance" of sentences, which frequently involves concepts from information retrieval and linear algebra.

### 1. Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF)

TF-IDF is a numerical statistic that reflects how important a word is to a document in a collection or corpus. It's often used to weigh words in sentences.

*   **Term Frequency (TF):** This measures how frequently a term (word) appears in a document (sentence). A higher TF means the word is more present in that specific sentence.
    $$TF(t, d) = \frac{\text{number of times term t appears in document d}}{\text{total number of terms in document d}}$$
    Here, 'document d' refers to a single sentence in our summarization context.

*   **Inverse Document Frequency (IDF):** This measures how rare or common a term is across the entire corpus (all sentences in the original text). A higher IDF means the term is rarer and thus potentially more informative.
    $$IDF(t, D) = \log \left( \frac{\text{total number of documents in corpus D}}{\text{number of documents d where term t appears}} \right)$$
    Here, 'corpus D' refers to the entire set of sentences in the input text. The logarithm is used to dampen the effect of IDF and prevent terms appearing in very few documents from dominating.

*   **TF-IDF Score:** The TF-IDF score for a term in a document is the product of its TF and IDF.
    $$TF-IDF(t, d, D) = TF(t, d) \times IDF(t, D)$$
    Sentences can then be represented as vectors where each dimension corresponds to a word, and its value is the word's TF-IDF score. The "importance" of a sentence can be derived from the sum or average of the TF-IDF scores of its constituent words.

### 2. Cosine Similarity

Once sentences are represented as numerical vectors (e.g., using TF-IDF), we need a way to measure how similar they are to each other. Cosine similarity is a popular metric for this. It measures the cosine of the angle between two non-zero vectors in a multi-dimensional space. The closer the cosine value is to 1, the more similar the vectors (and thus the sentences) are.

Given two sentence vectors, $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$:
$$\text{cosine_similarity}(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}) = \frac{\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B}}{\|\mathbf{A}\| \|\mathbf{B}\|}$$
Where:
*   $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B}$ is the dot product of vectors $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$.
*   $\|\mathbf{A}\|$ and $\|\mathbf{B}\|$ are the Euclidean magnitudes (or norms) of vectors $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ respectively.
    $$\|\mathbf{A}\| = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} A_i^2}$$

Cosine similarity is particularly useful in graph-based summarization methods where it defines the strength of the edges between sentence nodes.

### 3. Graph-based Ranking (e.g., TextRank)

TextRank (and LexRank) adapts the PageRank algorithm from web page ranking to text summarization.
1.  **Graph Construction:** Each sentence in the document becomes a node ($V_i$) in a graph.
2.  **Edge Weighting:** An edge is drawn between two sentences if they are sufficiently similar (e.g., their cosine similarity is above a certain threshold). The weight of the edge ($w_{ij}$) is typically their cosine similarity score.
3.  **Iterative Ranking:** An iterative algorithm is used to assign a score to each sentence based on the scores of sentences it is connected to. The intuition is that a sentence is important if it is similar to many other important sentences. The scoring formula for a sentence $V_i$ is:
    $$S(V_i) = (1 - d) + d \times \sum_{V_j \in In(V_i)} \frac{w_{ji}}{\sum_{V_k \in Out(V_j)} w_{jk}} S(V_j)$$
    Where:
    *   $S(V_i)$ is the score of sentence $V_i$.
    *   $d$ is the damping factor (a value between 0 and 1, typically 0.85), representing the probability of "jumping" to a random sentence.
    *   $In(V_i)$ is the set of sentences that point to $V_i$ (i.e., are similar to $V_i$).
    *   $Out(V_j)$ is the set of sentences that $V_j$ points to.
    *   $w_{ji}$ is the weight of the edge between $V_j$ and $V_i$ (e.g., cosine similarity).

This formula is iteratively applied until the scores converge. Sentences with higher final scores are considered more important and are selected for the summary.

## Advantages

*   **Factual Accuracy:** Since it only extracts existing sentences, the summary is guaranteed to be factually accurate and consistent with the original document. It doesn't "hallucinate" information.
*   **Grammatically Correct:** The extracted sentences are already grammatically correct and coherent within themselves, as they come directly from the source.
*   **Traceability:** It's easy to trace back any part of the summary to its original location in the source document, which is crucial for applications like legal or scientific summarization.
*   **Simplicity and Speed:** Extractive methods are generally simpler to implement and computationally less expensive than abstractive methods, making them faster for large volumes of text.
*   **No Need for Language Generation:** It avoids the complex challenges of natural language generation (NLG), such as ensuring fluency, coherence, and grammatical correctness in newly generated text.
*   **Robustness:** Less prone to generating nonsensical or irrelevant content compared to abstractive models, especially with limited training data.

## Disadvantages

*   **Lack of Cohesion and Coherence:** Simply concatenating extracted sentences might result in a summary that lacks smooth transitions, logical flow, or overall coherence, as sentences are taken out of their original context.
*   **Redundancy:** Extractive methods can sometimes select multiple sentences that convey very similar information, leading to redundancy in the summary.
*   **Inability to Rephrase:** It cannot rephrase sentences or synthesize information from multiple sentences into a single, more concise statement. This limits its ability to create truly succinct summaries.
*   **Limited to Existing Information:** The summary is strictly limited to the information explicitly stated in the original sentences. It cannot infer or generalize beyond what's written.
*   **Contextual Gaps:** Removing sentences might inadvertently remove crucial contextual information, making some extracted sentences harder to understand in isolation.
*   **Not Always Optimal Length:** It might be challenging to achieve a precise summary length, as sentences are discrete units. You might end up with a summary slightly longer or shorter than desired.

## Real World Applications

1.  **News Summarization:** News websites and aggregators use extractive summarization to provide quick overviews of articles. Users can read a short summary to decide if they want to delve into the full story, saving time and helping them stay informed across multiple sources.
2.  **Legal Document Review:** Lawyers and legal professionals deal with vast amounts of legal texts (contracts, case files, depositions). Extractive summarization helps them quickly identify key clauses, arguments, and facts, significantly speeding up the review process and ensuring critical information isn't missed.
3.  **Scientific Paper Abstracts:** Researchers often need to review numerous scientific papers. Extractive summarization can generate quick summaries of research articles, allowing scientists to rapidly assess the relevance and main findings of a paper before committing to reading the entire publication.
4.  **Customer Feedback Analysis:** Companies receive enormous volumes of customer feedback (reviews, support tickets, social media comments). Extractive summarization can distill these into key themes and common issues, helping businesses quickly understand customer sentiment and prioritize improvements.
5.  **Meeting Minutes Generation:** For long meetings, extractive summarization can automatically identify and extract the most important decisions, action items, and discussion points from a transcript, providing a concise record for attendees and those who couldn't attend.

## Python Example

This example demonstrates a basic extractive summarization using TF-IDF and Cosine Similarity to rank sentences, similar to the core idea behind graph-based methods like TextRank but simplified for clarity. We'll use `nltk` for sentence tokenization and `scikit-learn` for TF-IDF vectorization and cosine similarity.

```python
import nltk
from nltk.corpus import stopwords
from nltk.tokenize import sent_tokenize, word_tokenize
from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer
from sklearn.metrics.pairwise import cosine_similarity
import numpy as np

# Download necessary NLTK data (run once)
try:
    nltk.data.find('tokenizers/punkt')
except nltk.downloader.DownloadError:
    nltk.download('punkt')
try:
    nltk.data.find('corpora/stopwords')
except nltk.downloader.DownloadError:
    nltk.download('stopwords')

def extractive_summarizer(text, num_sentences=3):
    """
    Performs extractive text summarization using TF-IDF and Cosine Similarity.

    Args:
        text (str): The input text to summarize.
        num_sentences (int): The desired number of sentences in the summary.

    Returns:
        str: The summarized text.
    """
    # 1. Sentence Tokenization
    sentences = sent_tokenize(text)
    if len(sentences) <= num_sentences:
        return text # Return original text if it's already short enough

    # 2. Preprocessing and TF-IDF Vectorization
    # Initialize TF-IDF Vectorizer
    # We'll remove common English stopwords and consider unigrams (single words)
    stop_words = set(stopwords.words('english'))
    tfidf_vectorizer = TfidfVectorizer(stop_words=list(stop_words))

    # Fit and transform the sentences to get TF-IDF vectors
    # Each row in tfidf_matrix corresponds to a sentence
    tfidf_matrix = tfidf_vectorizer.fit_transform(sentences)

    # 3. Calculate Sentence Similarity (Cosine Similarity)
    # Compute the cosine similarity matrix between all sentence vectors
    # This matrix shows how similar each sentence is to every other sentence
    sentence_similarity_matrix = cosine_similarity(tfidf_matrix)

    # 4. Sentence Scoring (Simplified Graph-based approach)
    # For simplicity, we'll sum the similarity scores for each sentence.
    # A sentence with higher sum of similarities is considered more central/important.
    sentence_scores = np.sum(sentence_similarity_matrix, axis=1)

    # 5. Sentence Selection
    # Get the indices of the top 'num_sentences' highest-scoring sentences
    # We use argsort to get indices that would sort the array, then take the last 'num_sentences'
    # to get the indices of the highest scores.
    ranked_sentence_indices = np.argsort(sentence_scores)[-num_sentences:]

    # Sort the selected indices to maintain the original order of sentences in the summary
    ranked_sentence_indices.sort()

    # 6. Generate Summary
    summary_sentences = [sentences[i] for i in ranked_sentence_indices]
    summary = ' '.join(summary_sentences)

    return summary

# --- Dummy Dataset / Example Usage ---
document = """
Artificial intelligence (AI) is intelligence demonstrated by machines, unlike the natural intelligence displayed by humans and animals. Leading AI textbooks define the field as the study of "intelligent agents": any device that perceives its environment and takes actions that maximize its chance of successfully achieving its goals. Colloquially, the term "artificial intelligence" is often used to describe machines that mimic "cognitive" functions that humans associate with the human mind, such as "learning" and "problem-solving".

AI applications include advanced web search engines (e.g., Google Search), recommendation systems (used by YouTube, Amazon, and Netflix), understanding human speech (such as Siri and Alexa), self-driving cars (e.g., Waymo), generative AI (e.g., ChatGPT), and competing at the highest level in strategic game systems (such as chess and Go). As machines become increasingly capable, tasks considered to require "intelligence" are often removed from the definition of AI, a phenomenon known as the AI effect. For instance, optical character recognition is frequently excluded from the definition of AI, having become a routine technology.

Artificial intelligence was founded as an academic discipline in 1956, and in the years since has experienced several waves of optimism, followed by disappointment and the loss of funding (known as an "AI winter"), followed by new approaches, success, and renewed funding. AI research has received and continues to receive massive funding, and has seen a dramatic increase in capabilities since the 21st century.
"""

print("--- Original Document ---")
print(document)
print("\n" + "="*50 + "\n")

# Generate a summary with 3 sentences
summary_3_sentences = extractive_summarizer(document, num_sentences=3)
print("--- Extractive Summary (3 sentences) ---")
print(summary_3_sentences)
print("\n" + "="*50 + "\n")

# Generate a summary with 5 sentences
summary_5_sentences = extractive_summarizer(document, num_sentences=5)
print("--- Extractive Summary (5 sentences) ---")
print(summary_5_sentences)
print("\n" + "="*50 + "\n")

# Example with a very short text
short_text = "The cat sat on the mat. It was a fluffy cat. The mat was red."
print("--- Short Text Original ---")
print(short_text)
print("\n--- Short Text Summary (2 sentences) ---")
print(extractive_summarizer(short_text, num_sentences=2))
print("\n" + "="*50 + "\n")

# Example with text shorter than desired summary length
very_short_text = "Hello world. This is a test."
print("--- Very Short Text Original ---")
print(very_short_text)
print("\n--- Very Short Text Summary (3 sentences) ---")
print(extractive_summarizer(very_short_text, num_sentences=3))
print("\n" + "="*50 + "\n")
```

**Explanation of the Code:**

1.  **Import Libraries:** We import `nltk` for text processing, `TfidfVectorizer` and `cosine_similarity` from `sklearn` for numerical representation and similarity calculation, and `numpy` for array operations.
2.  **Download NLTK Data:** `punkt` tokenizer for sentence splitting and `stopwords` corpus are downloaded if not already present.
3.  **`extractive_summarizer` Function:**
    *   **Sentence Tokenization:** `sent_tokenize(text)` splits the input `text` into a list of individual sentences.
    *   **TF-IDF Vectorization:**
        *   `TfidfVectorizer` is initialized, configured to remove English stop words.
        *   `tfidf_vectorizer.fit_transform(sentences)` converts each sentence into a TF-IDF vector. The result is a sparse matrix where each row represents a sentence and each column represents a word, with values being their TF-IDF scores.
    *   **Cosine Similarity:** `cosine_similarity(tfidf_matrix)` calculates the pairwise cosine similarity between all sentence vectors, resulting in a square matrix. `matrix[i][j]` contains the similarity between sentence `i` and sentence `j`.
    *   **Sentence Scoring:** For simplicity, we sum the similarity scores for each sentence across all other sentences (`np.sum(sentence_similarity_matrix, axis=1)`). A higher sum indicates that a sentence is more similar to many other sentences, suggesting it's central to the document's theme.
    *   **Sentence Selection:**
        *   `np.argsort(sentence_scores)` returns the indices that would sort `sentence_scores` in ascending order.
        *   `[-num_sentences:]` selects the indices of the `num_sentences` with the highest scores.
        *   `ranked_sentence_indices.sort()` is crucial to ensure the selected sentences appear in the summary in their original order, preserving readability.
    *   **Summary Generation:** The selected sentences are retrieved from the original `sentences` list using their sorted indices and then joined together to form the final summary string.
4.  **Example Usage:** A sample `document` about AI is used to demonstrate the summarizer, generating summaries of different lengths. Edge cases like very short texts are also handled.

## Interview Questions

1.  **What is Extractive Text Summarization, and how does it differ from Abstractive Summarization?**
    *   **Answer:** Extractive summarization works by identifying and extracting the most important sentences directly from the original text to form a summary. It's like "copy-pasting" key sentences. The summary only contains content that was explicitly present in the source document.
    *   Abstractive summarization, on the other hand, generates new sentences and phrases that capture the main ideas of the original text. It involves understanding the text, synthesizing information, and then rephrasing it in a new, concise form, similar to how a human would write a summary.

2.  **Can you describe the typical pipeline for an extractive summarization system?**
    *   **Answer:** The typical pipeline involves:
        1.  **Preprocessing:** Tokenizing the text into sentences, then words, removing stop words, and potentially stemming/lemmatizing.
        2.  **Feature Extraction:** Representing each sentence numerically by extracting features like TF-IDF scores of its words, sentence position, presence of named entities, or similarity to the document title.
        3.  **Sentence Scoring:** Assigning an "importance" score to each sentence based on its extracted features. Common methods include frequency-based, TF-IDF based, or graph-based ranking (e.g., TextRank).
        4.  **Sentence Selection:** Choosing the top-N highest-scoring sentences (or sentences above a certain threshold) to be included in the summary. Diversity might also be considered here to avoid redundancy.
        5.  **Summary Generation:** Assembling the selected sentences, usually in their original order, to form the final summary.

3.  **What are some common techniques used to score sentences in extractive summarization?**
    *   **Answer:**
        *   **Frequency-based:** Sentences containing a high number of significant (non-stop) words are scored higher.
        *   **TF-IDF based:** Sentences with a higher aggregate TF-IDF score for their words are considered more important.
        *   **Position-based:** Sentences appearing at the beginning or end of paragraphs/documents are often given higher scores.
        *   **Graph-based Ranking (e.g., TextRank, LexRank):** Sentences are nodes in a graph, and edges represent similarity. An iterative algorithm (like PageRank) is used to score sentences based on their connectivity and the scores of connected sentences.
        *   **Topic Modeling (e.g., LSA, LDA):** Sentences that are highly representative of the dominant topics in the document are scored higher.

4.  **Explain the role of TF-IDF and Cosine Similarity in extractive summarization.**
    *   **Answer:**
        *   **TF-IDF:** It's used to quantify the importance of words within sentences and across the entire document. Words with high TF-IDF scores are considered more significant. Sentences can then be represented as vectors of these TF-IDF scores, allowing for numerical comparison.
        *   **Cosine Similarity:** Once sentences are represented as TF-IDF vectors, cosine similarity is used to measure the similarity between any two sentences. In graph-based methods, this similarity often forms the weights of the edges between sentence nodes, indicating how strongly two sentences are related. A higher cosine similarity means the sentences are more semantically alike.

5.  **What are the main advantages of using extractive summarization?**
    *   **Answer:** Advantages include factual accuracy (no hallucination), grammatical correctness (sentences are from the original text), traceability (easy to verify against the source), relative simplicity and speed of implementation, and avoiding the complexities of natural language generation.

6.  **What are the main disadvantages or limitations of extractive summarization?**
    *   **Answer:** Disadvantages include potential lack of coherence and smooth transitions between extracted sentences, possible redundancy if similar sentences are selected, inability to rephrase or synthesize information (leading to less concise summaries), and the risk of losing crucial context if connecting sentences are not selected.

7.  **When would you choose an extractive summarization approach over an abstractive one?**
    *   **Answer:** Extractive summarization is preferred when:
        *   Factual accuracy and traceability are paramount (e.g., legal, medical, scientific documents).
        *   Computational resources are limited, or speed is critical.
        *   The goal is to provide a quick overview rather than a perfectly fluent, human-like summary.
        *   There's a lack of large, high-quality parallel datasets (document-summary pairs) needed to train complex abstractive models.
        *   The risk of "hallucination" (generating factually incorrect information) must be minimized.

8.  **How can you evaluate the quality of an extractive summary?**
    *   **Answer:** Evaluation can be done using:
        *   **ROUGE (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation):** This is the most common metric. It measures the overlap of n-grams (sequences of words) between the generated summary and one or more human-written reference summaries. ROUGE-N (for n-gram overlap), ROUGE-L (for longest common subsequence), and ROUGE-S (for skip-bigram) are commonly used.
        *   **Human Evaluation:** Assess readability, coherence, informativeness, and factual accuracy by human judges. This is the gold standard but is expensive and time-consuming.
        *   **Precision/Recall/F1-score:** If sentences are labeled as "summary-worthy" or not, these metrics can be used to evaluate how well the system identifies relevant sentences.

9.  **What is TextRank, and how does it apply to extractive summarization?**
    *   **Answer:** TextRank is a graph-based ranking algorithm, inspired by Google's PageRank, used for various NLP tasks, including extractive summarization. For summarization, sentences are represented as nodes in a graph. Edges between nodes are weighted by the semantic similarity between the sentences (e.g., using cosine similarity of their TF-IDF vectors). The TextRank algorithm then iteratively calculates a "score" for each sentence, where sentences that are highly connected to many other important sentences receive higher scores. The top-scoring sentences are then selected for the summary.

10. **What are some challenges in implementing an effective extractive summarizer?**
    *   **Answer:**
        *   **Redundancy:** Ensuring the selected sentences don't repeat too much information. Diversity algorithms are often needed.
        *   **Coherence:** Maintaining a logical flow and smooth transitions when concatenating disparate sentences.
        *   **Context Preservation:** Ensuring that extracted sentences remain understandable even when taken out of their original context.
        *   **Optimal Length:** Determining the ideal number of sentences or words for a summary, which can vary based on the document and user needs.
        *   **Domain Specificity:** A summarizer trained on news articles might not perform well on legal documents without adaptation.
        *   **Evaluation:** ROUGE scores don't always perfectly correlate with human judgment of summary quality.
