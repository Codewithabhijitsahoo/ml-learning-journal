# Doc2Vec

## Overview
Doc2Vec, also known as Paragraph Vectors, is an unsupervised algorithm that learns fixed-size vector representations (embeddings) for variable-length pieces of text, such as sentences, paragraphs, or entire documents. It is an extension of the popular Word2Vec algorithm, designed to capture the semantic meaning and context of a document as a whole, rather than just individual words. These "document vectors" can then be used for various downstream tasks like similarity search, clustering, and classification.

## What Problem It Solves
Traditional methods for representing documents, such as Bag-of-Words (BoW) or TF-IDF, treat documents as collections of independent words, losing crucial information about word order and semantic relationships. While Word2Vec successfully addresses this for individual words, simply averaging the Word2Vec vectors of words within a document often results in a loss of nuance and context, leading to suboptimal document representations.

Doc2Vec solves this by:
1.  **Capturing Document-Level Semantics:** It learns a single, dense vector that encapsulates the overall meaning and topic of a document, considering the context of all words within it.
2.  **Handling Variable Lengths:** It produces fixed-size vectors regardless of the document's length, making it easy to use these vectors as features for machine learning models.
3.  **Improving Document Similarity:** It allows for more accurate measurement of semantic similarity between documents compared to traditional methods or simple Word2Vec averaging.

## How It Works
Doc2Vec extends Word2Vec by introducing a "document vector" (or paragraph vector) that represents the entire document. During training, this document vector is learned alongside the word vectors. There are two main architectures:

1.  **Distributed Memory Model of Paragraph Vectors (PV-DM):**
    *   This model is analogous to the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model in Word2Vec.
    *   It tries to predict a target word given its surrounding context words *and* the document vector.
    *   The document vector acts as a "memory" or a global context for the current document, influencing the prediction of words within it.
    *   Each document is assigned a unique ID, and a corresponding vector is learned for that ID. When training, the document vector is sampled from a matrix, concatenated with context word vectors, and used to predict the target word.

2.  **Distributed Bag-of-Words version of Paragraph Vectors (PV-DBOW):**
    *   This model is analogous to the Skip-gram model in Word2Vec.
    *   It ignores the context words and tries to predict random words from the document given *only* the document vector.
    *   It's simpler and often faster to train, as it doesn't need to consider word contexts.
    *   The document vector is essentially trained to represent the "bag of words" within its document.

In both models, the document vectors and word vectors are learned simultaneously through a neural network training process (typically using stochastic gradient descent and backpropagation), aiming to maximize the probability of predicting words given their context (and the document) or predicting words from the document given the document vector.

## Mathematical Intuition
The core idea behind Doc2Vec is to learn a vector representation for each document by training a neural network to predict words.

For **PV-DM**, the objective is to maximize the log probability of predicting the next word $w_t$ given a context of words $w_{t-k}, ..., w_{t+k}$ and the document vector $D$:

$$ \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=k}^{T-k} \log P(w_t | w_{t-k}, ..., w_{t+k}, D) $$

Here, $D$ is the unique document vector for the current document. This is typically modeled using a neural network where the input layer combines the document vector and context word vectors, and the output layer predicts the target word using a softmax function.

For **PV-DBOW**, the objective is simpler: maximize the log probability of predicting a random word $w_i$ from the document given only the document vector $D$:

$$ \frac{1}{|D|} \sum_{w_i \in D} \log P(w_i | D) $$

In both cases, the document vectors (and word vectors) are adjusted during training via backpropagation to improve these predictions. After training, each document will have a unique, fixed-size vector representation.

## Advantages
*   **Semantic Understanding:** Captures the semantic meaning and context of entire documents, not just individual words.
*   **Fixed-Size Representation:** Produces fixed-size vectors for documents of varying lengths, simplifying their use in machine learning models.
*   **Improved Performance:** Often outperforms simple averaging of Word2Vec vectors for document-level tasks.
*   **Versatile:** Useful for a wide range of NLP tasks such as document similarity, clustering, classification, and recommendation systems.
*   **Handles Word Order (PV-DM):** The PV-DM model implicitly considers word order through its context window, though not as explicitly as sequential models.

## Disadvantages
*   **Computationally Intensive:** Training can be slow and resource-heavy, especially on very large corpora, requiring significant computational power and time.
*   **Data Hungry:** Requires a substantial amount of training data to learn robust and meaningful document representations.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Performance can be sensitive to hyperparameters like vector size, window size, and training epochs.
*   **Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) Documents:** Documents not seen during training require an "inference" step to obtain their vectors, which can be slower and less accurate than directly trained vectors.
*   **Interpretability:** The learned dense vectors are abstract and lack direct human interpretability.

## Real World Applications
1.  **Content Recommendation Systems:** Recommending articles, news, movies, or products to users based on the semantic similarity between the content they have consumed and new items. For example, if a user reads several articles about AI, Doc2Vec can find other semantically similar AI articles.
2.  **Document Clustering and Classification:** Grouping similar documents together (e.g., categorizing news articles by topic, grouping customer feedback by common themes) or classifying documents into predefined categories (e.g., spam detection, sentiment analysis of reviews).
3.  **Plagiarism Detection:** Identifying documents that are semantically similar, even if they use different wording, by comparing their Doc2Vec embeddings. This can help detect copied content more effectively than simple keyword matching.

## Python Example

This example uses the `gensim` library to train a Doc2Vec model and infer document vectors.

```python
from gensim.models.doc2vec import Doc2Vec, TaggedDocument
from nltk.tokenize import word_tokenize
import nltk

# Download necessary NLTK data (if not already downloaded)
try:
    nltk.data.find('tokenizers/punkt')
except nltk.downloader.DownloadError:
    nltk.download('punkt')

# Sample documents
documents = [
    "I love machine learning and artificial intelligence.",
    "Machine learning is a fascinating field of study.",
    "Artificial intelligence is transforming the world.",
    "The cat sat on the mat.",
    "Dogs are loyal pets.",
    "Cats and dogs are common household animals."
]

# TaggedDocument format is required by Doc2Vec
# Each document needs a unique tag (e.g., an index)
tagged_data = [TaggedDocument(words=word_tokenize(_d.lower()), tags=[str(i)]) for i, _d in enumerate(documents)]

# Initialize and train the Doc2Vec model
# vector_size: dimensionality of the document vectors
# min_count: ignores all words with total frequency lower than this
# epochs: number of iterations (epochs) over the corpus
model = Doc2Vec(vector_size=20, min_count=1, epochs=100)
model.build_vocab(tagged_data)
model.train(tagged_data, total_examples=model.corpus_count, epochs=model.epochs)

# Infer a vector for a new document
new_document = "Artificial intelligence is a rapidly growing field."
new_doc_vector = model.infer_vector(word_tokenize(new_document.lower()))
print(f"Vector for '{new_document}':\n{new_doc_vector}\n")

# Find the most similar documents to the new document
# `model.dv` is the document vectors object
similar_docs = model.dv.most_similar([new_doc_vector], topn=2)
print(f"Most similar documents to '{new_document}':")
for tag, similarity in similar_docs:
    print(f"  Document '{documents[int(tag)]}' (Similarity: {similarity:.4f})")

# You can also get the vector for an existing document by its tag
existing_doc_vector = model.dv['0'] # Vector for the first document
print(f"\nVector for existing document 0 ('{documents[0]}'):\n{existing_doc_vector}")
```

## Interview Questions

1.  **How does Doc2Vec differ fundamentally from Word2Vec, and why is this difference important?**
    *   **Answer:** Word2Vec learns vector representations for individual words, capturing their semantic relationships. Doc2Vec, on the other hand, learns vector representations for entire documents (sentences, paragraphs, etc.). This difference is crucial because while Word2Vec helps understand word meaning, Doc2Vec provides a fixed-size representation for variable-length texts, enabling tasks like document similarity, clustering, and classification that require understanding the overall context and meaning of a document, which simple averaging of Word2Vec vectors often fails to capture effectively.

2.  **Explain the two main architectures of Doc2Vec (PV-DM and PV-DBOW) and their analogies to Word2Vec models.**
    *   **Answer:** The two main architectures are PV-DM (Distributed Memory) and PV-DBOW (Distributed Bag-of-Words).
        *   **PV-DM** is analogous to Word2Vec's CBOW (Continuous Bag-of-Words) model. It predicts a target word given its surrounding context words *and* a unique document vector. The document vector acts as a memory or a global context for the document.
        *   **PV-DBOW** is analogous to Word2Vec's Skip-gram model. It ignores context words and tries to predict random words from the document given *only* the document vector. It's generally simpler and faster to train.

3.  **What are the benefits of using Doc2Vec over simply averaging Word2Vec vectors for a document?**
    *   **Answer:** Averaging Word2Vec vectors can lose crucial information about word order, syntax, and the overall semantic flow of a document. Doc2Vec, by contrast, learns a dedicated document vector that is trained to represent the entire document's context and meaning. This allows Doc2Vec to capture more nuanced semantic relationships, handle polysemy better in the context of the document, and generally produce more robust and meaningful document representations, leading to better performance in downstream tasks like similarity search and classification.
